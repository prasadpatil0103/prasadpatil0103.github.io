The Business Question

  • Chi-square hypothesis testing to confirm the cash loan default rate difference is statistically real — not a sampling artifact — before presenting it as a policy recommendation.
  • A/B testing framework applied to evaluate acquisition strategy performance across customer cohorts.

Supply Chain Intelligence: $21M Revenue Risk Identified

Supply Chain Analytics & Demand Forecasting · 2026

The Business Question

A global retailer is experiencing a 57.3% late delivery rate across 180K orders spanning 23 regions. Leadership's assumption: it's a volume problem — too many orders, not enough capacity. The real question is whether that assumption is supported by data, and if not, what the data actually shows.

What the Data Showed

  • The assumption was wrong. Volume had no meaningful relationship to late delivery. The correlation between order volume and days_late was negligible.
  • First Class shipping fails 100% of the time — across all 27,814 shipments in that mode, across all regions and product types. Statistical testing confirms this is systematic, not random variation.
  • The root cause is a scheduling gap — not order volume, not product type, not region. Statistical validation (Kruskal-Wallis test) confirmed scheduling lead time as the primary driver. Everything else was ruled out.
  • This failure has been invisible for 37 months. The late delivery rate shows zero improvement over 3 years of data. No one had looked at the trend line.
  • High-value customers aren't protected. The top 25% of customers by spend — generating 58% of revenue — receive the exact same late rate as bottom-tier customers. No service differentiation exists.
  • One fix recovers risk globally. The top 3 products by revenue-at-risk hold the #1 position in all 23 regions simultaneously — meaning a single fulfilment scheduling correction addresses $21M in at-risk revenue everywhere.

How I Found It

  • Analyzed 180K orders across 3 years of data spanning 23 regions and 4 shipping modes — structured the dataset to enable apples-to-apples comparisons across modes, regions, and product types.
  • Used time-series SQL analysis to build a 37-month trend window — which revealed the flat late-delivery rate that nobody had plotted before.
  • Validated root cause with chi-square and Kruskal-Wallis hypothesis tests — ruling out order volume, product type, and region as explanations before confirming scheduling as the driver.
  • Demand forecasting with Prophet — accurate to within 2% of actual monthly order volume — gave operations a reliable forward view to plan capacity adjustments alongside the scheduling fix.
  • Findings presented in a Power BI dashboard built for executive and operations audiences — not for analysts.

Fraud Analytics: $3.08M Exposure Identified Across 590K Transactions

Financial Fraud Analytics · 2026

The Business Question

A financial institution has a 3.5% fraud rate across hundreds of thousands of transactions and no structured way to tell compliance teams where to focus. The question isn't "can we detect fraud?" — it's "which specific segments, time windows, and card types drive the exposure, and can we explain that in plain language to a non-technical reviewer?"

What the Analysis Found

  • Discover cards carry a 7.7% fraud rate — more than double Visa (3.5%). Statistical testing confirms this gap is real, not a sampling artifact. Card network is a statistically significant fraud predictor.
  • Hours 5–9 UTC are a high-risk window, peaking at 10.5% fraud rate at hour 7. Discover credit cards at hour 11 reach a 27.1% fraud rate — the single highest segment in the dataset.
  • $3.08M in fraudulent transactions identified and quantified across $79.7M total volume — giving compliance a dollar figure, not just a percentage.
  • SHAP made the model legible. TransactionAmt, card velocity, and transaction hour emerged as the top 3 fraud signals. A compliance analyst can now say "this transaction triggered the flag because of X" — not just "the model said so."
  • Model performance: The classifier catches 76 out of every 100 actual fraud cases — with 72% precision on flagged transactions. That's the difference between a compliance team that investigates meaningful leads and one chasing false positives all day. Baseline logistic regression caught only 59% comparably.

How I Found It

  • Analyzed 590K labeled transactions, structuring the data to enable segment-level fraud rate comparisons across card network, time of day, transaction amount, and device type.
  • SQL segment analysis using window functions surfaced the card network and time-window patterns — the $3.08M exposure figure came from aggregating flagged transactions, not from the model.
  • Built an XGBoost classifier — catching 76 out of every 100 actual fraud cases — to predict fraud probability per transaction, but treated model output as one input to the analysis, not the final answer.
  • Used SHAP to translate model signals into plain-language explanations for compliance stakeholders — so findings could be acted on without requiring technical literacy.

Fantasy Football: Can Historical Data Beat the Crowd's Projections?

IST 718 — Big Data Analytics · Syracuse University · 2026

The Business Question

Fantasy football platforms publish weekly player projections. But are those projections actually grounded in historical performance patterns — or are they largely intuition dressed up as data? This project tests whether a statistical model trained on 8 NFL seasons of player-week data produces more defensible projections.

What the Analysis Found

  • Prior season PPR is by far the strongest predictor — more predictive than age, position, or team context.
  • Position stability varies significantly: WRs are most consistent year-to-year (lagged correlation 0.69); QBs are most volatile (0.63) due to rushing variance that generic models miss.
  • One global model underserves every position. Position-specific models outperform: TEs most predictable (RMSE 39.98), QBs highest variance (RMSE 89.36). Treating all positions identically is analytically wrong.
  • Global model performance: Explains roughly half the variance in player fantasy output across 79K player-week observations — a meaningful improvement over gut feel, while being honest that individual player outcomes remain genuinely hard to predict.

2026 Projection Highlights

  • QB: Jaxson Dart at 362.5 projected PPR — model weights age trajectory over prior-year output.
  • RB: Breece Hall leads at 270.4 — consistent producers rewarded.
  • WR: Amon-Ra St. Brown tops at 263.3.
  • TE: Trey McBride leads at 241.2; Travis Kelce at 228.5 despite age penalty applied by the model.

What a Manager Should Do With This

  • Draft for consistency at WR and TE — these positions have the most predictable year-over-year patterns. Pay for proven production; variance is low enough to justify it.
  • Accept QB volatility as a structural reality — the data says rushing variance makes QBs genuinely hard to project. Don't overpay for a QB based on one outlier season.
  • Don't trust a single global projection — position-specific models consistently outperform combined ones. Any platform projecting all positions with one model is analytically wrong and the numbers prove it.

HOW I APPROACH ANALYSIS

Start With the Decision, Not the Data

Before opening a dataset, I ask: what decision does this analysis need to support, and who has to act on the answer? That question determines which metrics matter, which don't, and what "done" actually looks like.

Chase the Surprising Number

The most valuable finding is usually the one that contradicts what stakeholders assumed. I look for the 100% failure rate nobody noticed, the three-year flat trend everyone missed, the high-value customer getting the same treatment as everyone else.

Separate Statistical From Practical Significance

A statistically significant result isn't automatically worth acting on. In the supply chain analysis, every shipping mode difference was statistically significant — but only First Class had a 100% failure rate. That's the one that costs $21M. The others were noise that passed a p-value test. I report both the result and whether it's large enough to matter.

Make the Answer Legible to the Person Who Has to Act

An insight that lives in a Jupyter notebook isn't an insight yet. For the fraud project, AUC 0.81 is meaningless to a compliance reviewer — "catches 76 out of every 100 actual fraud cases, concentrated in Discover cards between 5–9 UTC" is actionable. I translate every finding into the language of its audience before it leaves my desk.

Know When the Data Can't Answer the Question

Sometimes the honest answer is "this dataset can't tell us that — here's what we'd need." Flagging the limits of an analysis is as valuable as the analysis itself. A confident wrong conclusion is worse than a clearly bounded one.

Analytical Perspective

The Difference Between a Report and an Insight — and Why It Took Me Two Years to Learn It

The Question That Changed How I Work

Two years at Tata Consultancy Services taught me how to produce reports. Two years at Syracuse taught me to ask a harder question first: does this report change what someone decides to do — or does it just confirm what they already believed? That distinction is the difference between analysis that matters and analysis that gets filed away.

The Lesson That Actually Stuck

Supply Chain Intelligence Platform. I went in expecting to find that late deliveries were caused by high order volume. The data said otherwise — First Class shipping was failing 100% of the time, the root cause was a scheduling gap, and the trend had been flat for 37 months without anyone noticing. That project taught me something I now apply to every analysis: the most valuable finding is usually the one that contradicts what everyone assumed. If your analysis just confirms what leadership already believed, you probably haven't dug deep enough.

Financial Fraud Detection. Building a fraud model with AUC 0.81 felt like a success until I realized that number means nothing to a compliance analyst. SHAP changed that — suddenly there was a way to say "this transaction was flagged because of this card type, at this hour, with this velocity pattern." The technical performance matters, but the insight only lands when the audience can follow the reasoning.

Crime Prediction in Syracuse (IST 707). 95% accuracy sounds impressive. But this project forced me to think about what happens when a model is wrong — in a domain where being wrong has real consequences for real people. Statistical performance and responsible deployment are separate questions, and conflating them is how technically accurate analyses cause harm.

"A finding tells you what the data says. An insight tells you what to do about it — and why it matters more than what everyone assumed before you ran the numbers."
