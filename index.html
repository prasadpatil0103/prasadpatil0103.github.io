NYC Taxi: Scalable Data Engineering Pipeline

Syracuse University | IST 782 | Data Engineering Track | Feb 2026 – Mar 2026

Problem

Raw NYC taxi data arrived as high-volume records with inconsistent formats and invalid entries, making it unusable for analytics or machine learning without a scalable transformation layer.

Approach — Medallion Architecture

  • Bronze Layer: Ingested raw NYC taxi data into Snowflake without transformation, preserving source fidelity for reprocessing and auditability.
  • Silver Layer: Used PySpark distributed processing across 5.7M+ records to apply schema standardization, quality validation, and cleansing rules.
  • Gold Layer: Built modular dbt models to produce curated datasets for demand, fare, pickup, and distance analysis.
  • Serving Layer: Exposed trusted Gold-layer datasets through FastAPI for dashboard and application consumption.

System Design View

This platform follows a modern data architecture: Ingestion → Distributed Processing → Data Warehouse → Transformation Layer → API Serving Layer. Each layer isolates responsibility, improving maintainability, scalability, and downstream reliability.

Results

  • Processed and transformed 5.7M+ records through a scalable ELT workflow.
  • Reduced invalid records before downstream analytics through Silver-layer quality enforcement.
  • Produced analytics-ready and ML-ready Gold datasets for demand, fare, and trip behavior analysis.
  • Enabled real-time access via FastAPI endpoints.

Engineering Decisions

  • Why Medallion? Separates raw data from validated data from business-ready data so source instability does not break downstream analytics.
  • Why PySpark? Distributed processing allows scalable validation and transformation across millions of records.
  • Why dbt? Modular, testable transformations are easier to govern and maintain than large ad hoc SQL scripts.
  • Why FastAPI? APIs expose trusted datasets without giving every consumer direct warehouse access.

Impact

Demonstrates production-grade data engineering: scalable compute, governed transformation, layered modeling, and API-based delivery.

Pipeline Architecture

End-to-end ELT architecture from raw ingestion through PySpark validation, dbt transformation, and FastAPI serving.

Program Reflection

From Mumbai to Syracuse: Two Years of Building, Breaking, and Rethinking Data

What I Expected Coming In

I joined the MS in Applied Data Science program with two years at Tata Consultancy Services behind me. I knew how to write SQL, build pipelines, and ship working systems. What I lacked was the why — the architectural reasoning, the statistical foundations, and the design principles that separate a brittle pipeline from a resilient one. I came to Syracuse to fill those gaps and to build systems at a higher level of rigor than on-the-job learning had given me.

What I Learned — Core Outcomes

Data engineering at scale came into focus through the Data Warehouse and Big Data Analytics courses. I went from writing SQL that worked to writing SQL designed to be tested, versioned, and trusted by someone who had never seen it before — using Snowflake, dbt, and the Medallion architecture as the practical toolkit.

Statistical and analytical reasoning was deepened in Quantitative Reasoning for Data Science. I learned to question my own results — to ask whether an accuracy metric actually means what I think it means, and whether the assumptions in a model hold for the specific problem at hand.

Machine learning stretched me furthest technically in Applied Machine Learning. I worked with ensemble methods, SVMs, time series models, and class imbalance techniques, and learned to evaluate models by precision and recall in context — not just headline accuracy.

Responsible AI was the outcome I least expected to find central to the curriculum. Multiple courses pushed me to treat ethical design as an engineering constraint, not an afterthought — a shift that permanently changed how I approach systems that affect real people.

Three Projects That Shaped My Thinking

NYC Taxi ELT Pipeline (IST 782). Building a full Medallion architecture — Bronze ingestion, PySpark Silver validation across 5.7M records, dbt Gold modeling, and FastAPI serving — forced me to justify every architectural decision in sequence. The process taught me that good data engineering is fundamentally about trust: downstream consumers rely on your system because they know exactly how it was built and tested.

Healthcare Hub (Fall 2025). Designing a Kimball star schema in Snowflake with automated dbt quality testing changed my view of data governance. Before this project, testing felt like overhead. After it, I understood that quality enforcement before data reaches a reporting layer is what makes the reporting layer trustworthy — not a nice-to-have.

Crime Prediction in Syracuse (IST 707). This project pushed me outside my technical comfort zone into ethical territory. Building a model that predicts geographic crime risk required thinking about bias, fairness, and harm in ways I had never formalized before. Implementing SMOTE, designing explainability into outputs, and writing a formal ethics section were as important as the 95% classification accuracy.

iConsult: JMA Wireless

My applied engagement at JMA Wireless in Fall 2025 was the most professionally formative experience of the program. I re-engineered their operational SQL and Python pipelines, analyzed 100K+ chamber records to surface bottlenecks, and delivered analytics that reduced SLA breaches by 40% and enabled $3.3M in projected annual savings. What the classroom could not replicate was the ambiguity — messy data, competing stakeholder priorities, compressed timelines. Navigating all of that and still shipping something that made a measurable difference was the real education.

Favorite Course & Best Surprises

Favorite course: Data Warehouse. It gave me the formal foundation I had been missing — Kimball methodology, conformed dimensions, star schema design — and structured the learning around design decisions rather than syntax. Every problem I had seen at TCS suddenly had a name and a principled solution.

Best part of the program: The project-based structure. Syracuse's iSchool requires you to build things, defend decisions, and produce work that can be evaluated on its design quality — not just whether it runs. That pressure drove more growth than any lecture.

Biggest surprise: How central ethical reasoning turned out to be. I expected a technically rigorous program. I did not expect it to push me to think as carefully about responsible deployment as about model accuracy. I leave convinced that the two are inseparable.

"Good data engineering is invisible when it works — the goal is systems so reliable that no one notices them, except when the business makes a better decision because the data was there, clean, on time, and trusted."
